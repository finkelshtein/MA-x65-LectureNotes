1  Loss distributions

Caution1.1 Definition: Loss

Loss is the value of an individual claim. It is modelled as a random variable.

Note1.2 Probability distribution

  • Let \((\Omega,\mathcal{A},\mathbb{P})\) be a probability space.

  • Recall that \[ \mathbb{P}(A)\in[0,1], \quad A\in \mathcal{A}; \tag{1.1}\] \[ \mathbb{P}(\emptyset)=0, \qquad \mathbb{P}(\Omega)=1. \tag{1.2}\]

  • A measurable function \(X:\Omega\to\mathbb{R}\) is called a random variable.

  • If \(X\) can take any value from an uncountable set, e.g. an interval of \(\mathbb{R}\), \(X\) is called a continuous random variable.

  • The cummulative distribution function (CDF) of \(X\) is a non-decreasing function \[ F_X(x)=\mathbb{P}(X\leq x)\in[0,1]. \tag{1.3}\]

  • Properties: \[ \lim_{x\to-\infty}F_X(x)=0, \tag{1.4}\] \[ \lim_{x\to+\infty}F_X(x)=1; \tag{1.5}\] \[ \mathbb{P}(a\leq X \leq b) = F_X(b)-F_X(a). \tag{1.6}\]

  • The survival function \(S_X\) is given by: \[ S_X(x):=\mathbb{P}(X>x)=1-F_X(x). \tag{1.7}\]

  • If \(F_X\) is differentiable (at almost all \(x\in\mathbb{R}\)), \(X\) is called an absolutely continuous random variable. In this case, the derivative of \(F_X\) is called the probability density function (PDF) (a.k.a. probability density) of \(X\): \[ f_X(x)=F'_X(x)\geq0. \tag{1.8}\]

  • Relations: \[ F_X(x)=\int_{-\infty}^x f_X(x)\,dx, \tag{1.9}\] \[ \mathbb{P}(a\leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f_X(x)\,dx, \tag{1.10}\] \[ S_X(x)=\int_x^{\infty} f_X(x)\,dx, \tag{1.11}\] \[ \int_{-\infty}^\infty f_X(x)\,dx =1. \tag{1.12}\]

  • We consider only absolutely continuous random variables, so we omit ``absolutely’’.

  • Note that, if \(X\) is a continuous random variable then \[ \mathbb{P}(X=a)=0\qquad \text{for each } a\in\mathbb{R}. \]

Note1.3 Moments

  • For any (measurable) \(g:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\), \(g(X):\Omega\to\mathbb{R}\) is also a random variable. The expectation (a.k.a. mean) of \(g(X)\) is \[ \mathbb{E}(g(X)) = \int_{\mathbb{R}} g(x) f_X(x)\,dx. \tag{1.13}\]

  • For a \(k\in\mathbb{N}\), the moment of order \(k\) of \(X\) is \[ m_k=m_{k,X}=\mathbb{E}(X^k)=\int_{\mathbb{R}} x^k \, f_X(x)\,dx. \tag{1.14}\]

  • In particular, the expectation of \(X\) is its first moment: \[ \mathbb{E}(X) = m_{1,X}=\int_{\mathbb{R}} x \, f_X(x)\,dx. \tag{1.15}\] It measures the average value of \(X\) (over many trials).

  • Remember that, for \(X,Y:\Omega\to\mathbb{R}\), \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\), \[ \mathbb{E}(\alpha X) = \alpha \, \mathbb{E}(X), \tag{1.16}\] \[ \mathbb{E}(X+Y)=\mathbb{E}(X)+\mathbb{E}(Y). \tag{1.17}\]

  • The central moment of order \(k\) of \(X\) is \[ \begin{aligned} \widetilde{m}_k&=\widetilde{m}_{k,X}=\mathbb{E}\bigl(X-\mathbb{E}(X)\bigr)^k\notag\\&=\int_\mathbb{R} \bigl(x - \mathbb{E}(X)\bigr)^k \, f_X(x)\,dx. \end{aligned} \tag{1.18}\]

  • The variance of a random variable \(X\) is its second central moment: \[ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Var}(X):&= \mathbb{E}\bigl((X-\mathbb{E}(X))^2\bigr)\\ & = \mathbb{E}(X^2)-\bigl(\mathbb{E}(X)\bigr)^2=m_{2,X}-(m_{1,X})^2 \end{aligned} \tag{1.19}\] It measures the spread or dispersion of the values of \(X\) (over many trials). Note that \[ \mathrm{Var}(\alpha X) = \alpha^2 \mathrm{Var} (X), \quad \alpha\in\mathbb{R}. \tag{1.20}\]

  • The skewness of \(X\) is its third central moment: \[ \mathrm{skew}(X):= \mathbb{E}\bigl((X-\mathbb{E}(X))^3\bigr). \tag{1.21}\] It measures the asymmetry of the distribution of \(X\) about its mean. Note that the negative skew means that the left tail of \(f_X(x)\) is ``longer’’, i.e. the graph of \(f_X(x)\) is inclined to the right; whereas positive skew means that the graph of \(f_X(x)\) is inclined to the left.

  • The standard deviation of \(X\) is \[ \sigma(X):=\sqrt{ \mathrm{Var}(X)}\geq0. \tag{1.22}\]

  • The coefficient of skewness of \(X\) is \[ \gamma_X := \frac{\widetilde{m}_{3,X}}{\sigma(X)^3}=\mathbb{E}\biggl(\Bigl(\frac{X-\mathbb{E}(X)}{\sigma(X)}\Bigr)^3\biggr)\in\mathbb{R}. \tag{1.23}\]

  • The moment generator function (MGM) is \[ M_X(t)=\mathbb{E}(e^{tX})=\int_{\mathbb{R}} e^{tx} \, f_X(x)\,dx\geq0. \tag{1.24}\]

  • Relation: \[ M_X(t)=1+\sum_{k=1}^\infty\frac{m_k}{k!}t^k. \tag{1.25}\] and \[ m_{k,X} = M_X^{(k)}(0) \tag{1.26}\]

  • A random variable is uniquely determined by either of CDF, PDF, MGF.

Note1.4 Conditional characteristics

  • For random variables \(X\) and \(Y\), the \(\mathbb{E}(X\mid Y)\) (of \(X\) given \(Y\)) is a random variable measurable with respect to the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by \(Y\) (i.e. by all subsets \(\{Y\leq a\}\subset\Omega\) for \(a\in\mathbb{R}\)) such that \[ \mathbb{E}\bigl(\mathbb{E}(X\mid Y)\bigr) = \mathbb{E}(X). \tag{1.27}\]

  • In particular, if \(Y_1,\ldots,Y_n\) makes a partition of \(\Omega\) (i.e. their disjoint union is \(\Omega\)), then \[ \mathbb{E}(X)=\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}(X\mid Y_i)\mathbb{P}(Y_i). \tag{1.28}\]

  • The conditional variance of \(X\) given \(Y\) is the random variable \[ \mathrm{Var}(X\mid Y) = \mathbb{E}\Bigl( \bigl(X- \mathbb{E}(X\mid Y)\bigr)^2 \Bigm\vert Y \Bigr) \tag{1.29}\]

  • The law of total variance is that \[ \mathrm{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}\bigl(\mathrm{Var}(X\mid Y)\bigr) + \mathrm{Var}\bigl(\mathbb{E}(X\mid Y)\bigr). \tag{1.30}\]

Note1.5 Covariance and correlation

  • For random variables \(X,Y:\Omega\to\mathbb{R}\), their covariance is defined by \[ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{cov}(X,Y)&=\mathbb{E}\bigl((X-\mathbb{E}(X))(Y-\mathbb{E}(Y))\bigr)\\ &=\mathbb{E}(XY)-\mathbb{E}(X)\mathbb{E}(Y). \end{aligned} \tag{1.31}\]

  • Properties: \[ \mathrm{cov}(X,X)=\mathrm{Var}(X), \tag{1.32}\] \[ \mathrm{cov}(\alpha X, \beta Y)=\alpha\beta \mathrm{cov}(X,Y), \tag{1.33}\] \[ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{cov}(X_1+X_2,Y_1+Y_2)&=\mathrm{cov}(X_1,Y_1)+\mathrm{cov}(X_1,Y_2)\notag\\&\quad+\mathrm{cov}(X_2,Y_1)+\mathrm{cov}(X_2,Y_2). \end{aligned} \]

  • The correlation of \(X\) and \(Y\) is defined by \[ \mathrm{corr}(X,Y)=\frac{\mathrm{cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma(X)\sigma(Y)}\in[-1,1]. \tag{1.34}\]

  • Two random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) are called uncorrelated if \(\mathrm{cov}(X,Y)=0\).

    In particular, if \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent then \(\mathbb{E}(XY)=\mathbb{E}(X)\mathbb{E}(Y)\), and hence they are uncorrelated. The opposite, however, is not necessary true.

  • If \(X\) and \(Y\) are uncorrelated then \[ \mathrm{Var}(X+Y)=\mathrm{Var}(X)+\mathrm{Var}(Y). \tag{1.35}\]

  • For general \(X\) and \(Y\), \[ \mathrm{Var}(X+Y)=\mathrm{Var}(X)+\mathrm{Var}(Y)+2\mathrm{cov}(X,Y). \]

Note1.6 The exponential distribution

A random variable \(X:\Omega\to{\mathbb{R}}\) has the exponential distribution with parameter \(\lambda>0\) if its CDF is \[ F(x)=1-e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x\geq0. \tag{1.36}\]

Here and below, when we write a restriction on \(x\), we mean that function is \(0\) otherwise, i.e. here \(F(x)=0\) for \(x<0\).

Then the PDF is \[ f(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x\geq0. \tag{1.37}\]

Next, \[ \mathbb{E}(X)=\frac1{\lambda}, \tag{1.38}\] \[ \mathrm{Var}(X)=\frac1{\lambda^2}= \bigl( \mathbb{E}(X) \bigr)^2, \tag{1.39}\] \[ \mathbb{E}(X^2)=\frac2{\lambda^2}=2\bigl( \mathbb{E}(X) \bigr)^2. \tag{1.40}\] The MGM if defined for \(t<\lambda\) only: \[ M(t)=\biggl(1-\frac{t}{\lambda}\biggr)^{-1}=\frac{\lambda}{\lambda-t}. \] The notation is \[ X\sim Exp(\lambda). \tag{1.41}\]

Note1.7 The gamma distribution

A random variable \(X:\Omega\to{\mathbb{R}}\) has the gamma distribution with parameters \(\alpha>0\) and \(\lambda>0\) if its PDF is \[ f(x)=\frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)}x^{\alpha-1} e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x\geq0. \tag{1.42}\] Here \[ \Gamma(\alpha):=\int_0^\infty t^{\alpha-1}e^{-t}\,dt, \quad \alpha>0 \tag{1.43}\] is the gamma function.

The following formulas hold: \[ \mathbb{E}(X)=\frac{\alpha}{\lambda}, \tag{1.44}\] \[ \mathbb{E}(X^2)=\frac{\alpha+\alpha^2}{\lambda^2}, \tag{1.45}\] \[ \mathrm{Var}(X)=\frac{\alpha}{\lambda^2}, \tag{1.46}\] \[ M_X(t)=\biggl(1-\frac{t}{\lambda}\biggr)^{-\alpha}=\frac{\lambda^\alpha}{(\lambda-t)^\alpha}, \quad t<\lambda. \tag{1.47}\] The notation is \[ X\sim \Gamma(\alpha,\lambda). \]

Note1.8 The normal distribution

A random variable \(X:\Omega\to{\mathbb{R}}\) has the normal distribution with the mean \(\mu\) and the variance \(\sigma^2\) if \[ f_X(x)=\frac1{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac1{2\sigma^2}(x-\mu)^2}. \tag{1.48}\] Indeed, \[ \mathbb{E}(X)=\mu, \quad \mathrm{Var}(X)=\sigma^2. \tag{1.49}\] The MGF is defined now everywhere: \[ M_X(t)=e^{\mu t +\frac12 \sigma^2 t^2}, \quad t\in{\mathbb{R}}. \tag{1.50}\] Notation \[ X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2). \]

Note1.9 The lognormal distribution

A random variable \(X:\Omega\to{(0,\infty)}\) has the lognormal distribution with parameters \(\mu\) and \(\sigma^2\) iff \[ \ln X \sim N(\mu,\sigma^2). \tag{1.51}\] Equivalently, if \(Z\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\), then \(X=e^Z\).

Notation: \[ X\sim\ln N (\mu,\sigma^2). \]

Then \[ \mathbb{E}(X)= e^{\mu+\frac12\sigma^2}, \tag{1.52}\] \[ \mathrm{Var}(X)=e^{2\mu+\sigma^2}\left( e^{\sigma^2} -1\right), \tag{1.53}\] \[ f_X(x)= \frac{1}{x\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\exp\biggl(- \frac{(\ln x -\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\biggr) \tag{1.54}\]

Note1.10 Pareto distribution

A random variable \(X\) has the two-parameter Pareto distribution with parameters \(\alpha>0\) and \(\lambda>0\), if \[ F_X(x)=1-\biggl(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda+x}\biggr)^\alpha, \qquad x\geq0. \tag{1.55}\] Notation \(X\sim Pa(\alpha,\lambda)\).

Then \[ f_X(x)=\frac{\alpha\lambda^\alpha}{(\lambda+x)^{\alpha+1}}, \tag{1.56}\] and, {for \(\alpha>1\)}, \[ \mathbb{E}(X)=\frac{\lambda}{\alpha-1} \tag{1.57}\] A modification of the Pareto distribution is the Burr distribution with additional parameter \(\gamma>0\) \[ F_{Burr}(x)=F_{Pareto}(x^\gamma)=1-\biggl(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda+x^\gamma}\biggr)^\alpha. \tag{1.58}\]

Note1.11 The Weibull distribution

For \(a>0\), \(b>0\), we denote \(X\sim W(a,b)\) iff \[ F_X(x)=1-e^{-a x^b}, \tag{1.59}\] \[ f_X(x)=ab x^{b-1}e^{-a x^b}, \tag{1.60}\] \[ \mathbb{E}(X)=\Gamma\biggl(1+\frac{1}{b}\biggr)a^{-\frac{1}{b}}. \tag{1.61}\]

Note1.12 Likelihood

Let \(X:\Omega\to{\mathbb{R}}\) be a random variable whose distribution depends on a parameter \(\theta\in{\mathbb{R}}\).

Suppose that we observe the data \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\) which is the output of this random variable \(X\) in course of \(n\) independent trials.

In other words, we can say that we observe that i.i.d.r.v. \(X_1,\ldots, X_n\) with \(X_i\sim X\), \(1\leq i\leq n\), take certain values: \(X_1=x_1,\ldots, X_n=x_n\).

The likelihood, or likelihood function, is the function \(\mathcal{L}(\theta)=\mathcal{L}(\theta\mid x_1,\ldots,x_n)\) of the unknown parameter \(\theta\) (given the observed data \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\)) which is equal to:

  • (if \(X\) is a discrete random variable) the probability to observe this data (given the value of the parameter \(\theta\)): \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal{L}(\theta)&=\mathcal{L}(\theta\mid x_1,\ldots,x_n)\\ :&=\mathbb{P}(X_1=x_1,\ldots,X_n=x_n\mid \theta) \\&= \mathbb{P}(X_1=x_1\mid \theta)\ldots \mathbb{P}(X_n=x_n\mid \theta). \end{aligned} \tag{1.62}\]

  • (if \(X\) is a continuous random variable with the PDF \(f_X(x)=f_X(x\mid\theta)\)) \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal{L}(\theta)&=\mathcal{L}(\theta\mid x_1,\ldots,x_n)\\ :&=f_X(x_1\mid\theta)f_X(x_2\mid\theta)\ldots f_X(x_n\mid\theta). \end{aligned} \tag{1.63}\]

Note1.13 Maximum likelihood estimator

The maximum likelihood estimator\(\theta_*\) of the parameter \(\theta\) is the argument of the maximum of the likelihood function: \[ \theta_*=\mathop{\mathrm{argmax}}_\theta\mathcal{L}(\theta), \tag{1.64}\] that means that \[ \mathcal{L}(\theta_*) = \max_{\theta}\mathcal{L}(\theta). \tag{1.65}\]

The standard approach to find \(\theta_*\) is to consider the log-likelihood function \[ L(\theta):=L(\theta\mid x_1,\ldots,x_n)=\ln \mathcal{L}(\theta\mid x_1,\ldots,x_n). \tag{1.66}\]

Thus, in the discrete case, \[ L(\theta) = \ln \mathbb{P}(X_1=x_1\mid \theta)+ \ldots + \ln \mathbb{P}(X_n=x_n\mid \theta), \tag{1.67}\] whereas, in the continuous case \[ L(\theta) = \ln f_X(x_1\mid\theta)+ \ldots + \ln f_X(x_n\mid\theta), \tag{1.68}\]

Then \(\theta_*\) is the point of maximum for both \(\mathcal{L}\) and \(L\): \[ \theta_*=\mathop{\mathrm{argmax}}_\theta L(\theta)=\mathop{\mathrm{argmax}}_\theta\mathcal{L}(\theta). \tag{1.69}\]

Warning1.14 Example: Discrete case

Let \(X\sim Po(\lambda)\), i.e. \[ \mathbb{P}(X=k)=\frac{\lambda^k}{k!}e^{-\lambda}, \quad k\geq0. \] Suppose we have a sample of \(n\) values of \(X\): \(k_1,\ldots,k_n\), and we want to estimate \(\lambda\) (here \(\theta=\lambda\)). Then \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal{L}(\lambda)&=\mathbb{P}(X=k_1\mid\lambda)\ldots \mathbb{P}(X=k_n\mid\lambda)\\ & = \frac{\lambda^{k_1}}{k_1!}e^{-\lambda}\cdot\ldots\cdot \frac{\lambda^{k_n}}{k_n!}e^{-\lambda}\\ & = \underbrace{\frac{1}{k_1!\ldots k_n!}}_{=: c>0}\lambda^{k_1+\ldots+k_n}e^{-\lambda n}, \end{aligned} \] and therefore, \[ \begin{aligned} L(\lambda)&=\ln\mathcal{L}(\lambda) \\& = \ln c + (k_1+\ldots+k_n)\ln\lambda-\lambda n. \end{aligned} \] Then \[ L'(\lambda) = \frac{k_1+\ldots+k_n}{\lambda}-n, \] and hence, \(L'(\lambda)=0\) iff \[ {\lambda = \frac{k_1+\ldots+k_n}{n}}. \]

Since \[ L''(\lambda) = (L'(\lambda))'=-\frac{k_1+\ldots+k_n}{\lambda^2}<0, \] the found value \(\lambda_* = \frac{k_1+\ldots+k_n}{n}\) is the point of maximum of \(L\), hence, it is the maximum likelihood estimator for the parameter \(\lambda\).

Tip1.15 Remark: Relation to the LLN

Note that, by the law of large numbers (LLN), if \(X_i\sim X\sim Po(\lambda)\), \(i\in\mathbb{N}\), then \[ \frac{X_1+\ldots+X_n}{n} \to \mathbb{E}(X)=\lambda, \qquad n\to\infty. \] In other words, the maximum likelihood estimator converges to the theoretical value is the size of the sample converges to infinity.

Warning1.16 Example: Continuous case

Let \(X\sim Exp(\lambda)\). Suppose we have a sample of \(n\) values of \(X\): \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\). Then \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal{L}(\lambda) &= f_X(x_1\mid \lambda)\ldots f_X(x_n\mid \lambda)\\ &=\lambda e^{-\lambda x_1}\ldots \lambda e^{-\lambda x_n}= \lambda^n e^{-\lambda(x_1+\ldots +x_n)}. \end{aligned} \]

Therefore, \[ \begin{aligned} L(\lambda)&= \ln \mathcal L(\lambda) \\&= n \ln \lambda - \lambda(x_1+\ldots +x_n), \end{aligned} \] so \(L'(\lambda)=0\) iff \[ \begin{gathered} \frac{n}{\lambda} -(x_1+\ldots +x_n)=0,\\ {\lambda_*= \frac{n}{x_1+\ldots +x_n}}. \end{gathered} \] Note that \(L''(\lambda)=-\dfrac{n}{\lambda^2}\), in particular, \(L''(\lambda_*)<0\), i.e. \(\lambda_*\) is indeed the maximum likelihood estimator for the parameter \(\lambda\).

Finally, note that if \(X_i\sim X\sim Exp(\lambda)\), then, by LLN, \[ \frac{X_1+\ldots+X_n}{n}\to \mathbb{E}(X)=\frac{1}{\lambda}, \qquad n\to\infty. \]

Note1.17 The method of moments

If the number of parameters is bigger than \(1\), the maximum likelihood estimation may be challenging as one would need to find the point of maximum for a function of several variables (though it can be still effectively done numerically).

Another method to estimate unknown parameters of the distribution is the method of moments. Namely, assuming that we have a sample of \(n\) values \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\) of a random variable \(X\) which is believed to be followed a probability distribution which depends on \(k\) unknown parameters \(\theta=(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_k)\).

Then, we consider the first \(k\) moments of the random variable \(X\) (i.e. the moments of the population): \[ m_j = \mathbb{E}(X^j\mid \theta), \qquad 1\leq j\leq k. \]

Next, for the given data \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\), we consider \(k\) averaged moments (i.e. the moments of the sample): \[ \overline{m}_j = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{l=1}^n x_l ^j, \qquad 1\leq j\leq k. \]

After this, we need to equate \(k\) quantities: \[ m_j = \overline{m}_j, \qquad 1\leq j\leq k, \] to determine \(k\) unknown parameters \(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_k\).
