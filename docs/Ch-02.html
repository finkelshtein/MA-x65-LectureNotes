2  Risk models

2.1 Definition: Loss

Recall that (see Definition 1.1), a loss is the value of an individual claim. A risk is a source of potential loss (e.g. a policy or another short-term insurer contract). Each risk may give a rise to a loss (during the period of contract) or it may produce no loss over the period.

2.2 Risks and the individual risk model

Suppose that an insurance company has a portfolio with \(n\) policies, hence, \(n\) risks: \(i=1,\ldots,n\). Let \(Y_i\) represents the total loss from policy \(i\) during the period.

An individual risk model describes the aggregated loss \(S\) from all policies:

\[ S=Y_1+\ldots+Y_n. \]

However, if policy \(i\) didn’t rise a loss, then we should take \(Y_i=0\). It is more convenient to rewrite

\[ Y_i=J_i \widetilde{X}_i, \]

where \(J_i=0\), if policy \(i\) didn’t rise a claim, \(J_i=1\) if policy \(i\) raised a claim, and then \(\widetilde{X}_i\) is the value of the claim (the loss), distributed randomly according e.g. to one of the loss distributions we studied in the previous chapter.

Therefore, effectively, \(S\) is the sum of those \(\widetilde{X}_i\) where the corresponding \(J_i=1\). Consider the total number of policies where claims were raised:

\[ N=\sum_{i=1}^n J_i. \tag{2.1}\]

Now, \(N\) is a random number, and \(S\) is a sum of \(N\) random \(\widetilde{X}\)’s (recall, only whose \(\widetilde{X}\)’s where the corresponding \(J\)’s are not zero).

2.3 The collective risk model

To avoid complicated notations, we rewrite the above as a collective risk model. Namely, let \(N\) be the number of claims raised during the time period (i.e. the number of policies which raised claims). We may treat \(N\) as a random variable

\[ N:\Omega\to\mathbb{Z}_+:=\mathbb{N}\cup\{0\}. \]

Assume that all raised claims, \(X_1\), , \(X_N\) are i.i.d.r.v. (independent identically distributed random variables), and that \(N\) is independent on \(X_i\).

The aggregated claim (a.k.a. total loss) is then

\[ S=S_N:=X_1+\ldots+X_N, \tag{2.2}\]

where \(S:=0\) if \(N=0\).

(Note that here \(X\)’s are \(\widetilde{X}\)’s with nonzero \(J\)’s from the previous subsection!)

Then

\[ F_S(x)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty \mathbb{P}(N=n)\, \mathbb{P}(S_N\leq x \mid N=n). \tag{2.3}\]

Next,

\[ \mathbb{E}(S) = \mathbb{E}(N)\, \mathbb{E}(X); \tag{2.4}\] \[ \mathrm{Var}(S) = \mathbb{E}(N)\, \mathrm{Var}(X) + \mathrm{Var}(N)\, \left( \mathbb{E}(X) \right)^2; \tag{2.5}\] \[ M_S(t) = M_N\left( \ln M_X(t) \right). \tag{2.6}\]

2.4 How to model \(N\)

Recall that, in (2.1), \(J_i\in\{0,1\}\) is random. Suppose that all \(J\)’s are i.i.d.r.v. with

\[ \mathbb{P}(J=1)=p\in[0,1]. \]

Then

\[ N=\sum_{i=1}^n J_i \sim Bin(n,p), \]

i.e. \(N\) is a binomial random variable with

\[ \mathbb{P}(N=k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k (1-p)^{n-k}, \tag{2.7}\]

where \(k=0,\ldots, n\). Note that then \(\mathbb{E}(N)=np\).

If \(n\) is large (there are a lot of policies), and if \(p\) is small (if the probability of a claim per policy were large, an insurer would need to charge very high premiums, and the insurance would no longer be viable), and if, however, \(\lambda:=np=\mathbb{E}(N)\) is moderate, (so that, in average, there is a non-negligible number of claims), then \(N\) can be approximately modelled as a Poisson r.v. with the parameter \(\lambda\):

\[ N\sim Po(\lambda). \]

Of course, the assumption that all \(J_i\) have the same distributions, i.e. that all risks (policies) have equal chances to give rise to claims is very restrictive. In particular, for \(N\sim Po(\lambda)\), we have by (?eq-EVarPois) that \(\mathbb{E}(N)=\mathrm{Var}(N)=\lambda\), whereas real insurance data shows that often \(\mathrm{Var}(N)>\mathbb{E}(N)\).

Instead, we may assume that probabilities \(p_i= \mathbb{P}(J_i=1)\) are small but different, and consider \(\Lambda=\sum_i p_i\). It is convenient to model \(\Lambda\) as a random variable itself. In particular, if \(\Lambda\sim \Gamma(k,\beta)\) for some \(k\in\mathbb{N}\), \(\beta>0\), then \(N\) has negative binomial distribution with parameters \(k\) and \(p:=\frac{\beta}{1+\beta}\in(0,1)\), i.e.~\(N\sim NB(k,p)\) with \[ \mathbb{P}(N=n)=\binom{n+k-1}{n}p^k(1-p)^n, \quad n\in\mathbb{Z}_+. \tag{2.8}\]

2.5 The compound Poisson distribution (cPd)

Let \(N\sim Po(\lambda)\), i.e. for \(n\in\mathbb{Z}_+\),

\[ \mathbb{P}(N=n)=\frac{\lambda^n}{n!}e^{-\lambda}, \qquad n\in\mathbb{Z}_+. \]

Let \(X_1, \ldots, X_N\) be i.i.d.r.v. Then \(S=X_1+\ldots+X_N\) is said to have the compound Poisson distribution (cPd) with the parameter \(\lambda\). Formulas: \[ \mathbb{E}(S)=\lambda m_{1,X}, \tag{2.9}\] \[ \mathrm{Var}(S)=\lambda m_{2,X}, \tag{2.10}\] \[ \mathrm{skew}(S)=\lambda m_{3,X}, \tag{2.11}\] \[ M_S(t)= \exp\bigl( \lambda (M_X(t)-1) \bigr). \tag{2.12}\]

An important property is that a sum of independent compound Poisson r.v. is again a compound Poisson r.v. Namely, let \(S_1,\ldots, S_m\) be independent compound Poisson random variables with parameters \(\lambda_i\) and CDF of the single claims \(F_i(x)\), \(1\leq i\leq m\), i.e.

\[ S_i = X_1^{(i)}+\ldots+X_{N_i}^{(i)}, \]

where, for each \(i\), \(\{X_j^{(i)}\}\) are i.i.d.r.v. with CDF \(F_i\), and

\[ N_i\sim Po(\lambda_i). \]

Moreover, let \(\{X_j^{(i)}, N_i\}\) be also mutually independent for different \(i\).

Then

\[ \mathbf{S}:=S_1+\ldots+S_m \]

is a compound Poisson r.v. with the parameter

\[ \Lambda=\lambda_1+\ldots+\lambda_m \tag{2.13}\]

and the CDF of the single claim is

\[ F(x)=\frac{1}{\Lambda} \bigl( \lambda_1 F_1(x)+\ldots+\lambda_m F_m(x) \bigr), \tag{2.14}\]

i.e. \(S=Y_1+\ldots+Y_N\) for (some) i.i.d.r.v. \(\{Y_j\}\) with the CDF \(F(x)\), and \(N\sim Po(\Lambda)\).

2.6 The compound binomial distribution

Let \(N\sim Bin(n,p)\), \(n\in\mathbb{N}\), \(p\in[0,1]\), i.e. (2.7) holds, and also \(\mathbb{E}(N) = np\), \(\mathrm{Var}(N)=np(1-p)\), \(M_N(t)=(pe^t+1-p)^n\). Let \(X_1, \ldots, X_N\) be i.i.d.r.v. Then \(S=X_1+\ldots+X_N\) is said to have the compound binomial distribution. Formulas:

\[ \mathbb{E}(S)=np m_{1,X}, \tag{2.15}\] \[ \mathrm{Var}(S)= np m_{2,X} -np^2 m_{1,X}^2, \tag{2.16}\] \[ M_S(t) = (pM_X(t)+1-p)^n, \tag{2.17}\] \[ \mathrm{skew}(S)=np m_{3,X}-3np^2m_{2,X}m_{1,X} +2np^3m_{1,X}^3. \tag{2.18}\]

2.7 The compound negative binomial distribution

Let \(N\sim NB(k,p)\), \(k\in\mathbb{N}\), \(p\in[0,1]\), i.e. (2.8) holds. Then, for \(q=1-p\), \(\mathbb{E}(N)=\frac{kq}{p}\), \(\mathrm{Var}(N)=\frac{kq}{p^2}\), \(M_N(t)=p^k(1-qe^t)^{-k}\). Let \(X_1, \ldots, X_N\) be i.i.d.r.v. Then \(S=X_1+\ldots+X_N\) is said to have the compound negative binomial distribution. Formulas: \[ \mathbb{E}(S)=\frac{kq}{p}m_{1,X}, \tag{2.19}\] \[ \mathrm{Var}(S)=\frac{kq}{p}m_{2,X}+\frac{kq^2}{p^2}m_{1,X}^2, \tag{2.20}\] \[ M_S(t)=\frac{p^k}{(1-qM_X(t))^k}, \tag{2.21}\] \[ \mathrm{skew}(S)=\frac{3kq^2m_{1,X}m_{2,X}}{p^2}+\frac{2kq^3m_{1,X}^3}{p^3}+\frac{kqm_{3,X}}{p}. \tag{2.22}\]

2.8 Reinsurance

  • \(X\) is the gross claim amount random variable

  • \(Y\) is the net claim amount (paid by the main insurer after receiving the reinsurance recovery)

  • \(Z\) is the amount paid by the reinsurer

2.9 Example: Proportional reinsurance

For \(0<\alpha<1\), we set

\[ Y=\alpha X, \qquad Z=(1-\alpha)X. \tag{2.23}\]

Then

\[ \begin{alignat*}{2} \mathbb{E}(Y)&=\alpha\mathbb{E}(X), &\qquad \mathbb{E}(Z)&=(1-\alpha)\mathbb{E}(X),\\ \mathrm{Var}(Y)&=\alpha^2\mathrm{Var}(X), &\qquad \mathrm{Var}(Z)&=(1-\alpha)^2\mathrm{Var}(X). \end{alignat*} \]

2.10 Example: Excess of loss reinsurance

The insurer pays in full up to an amount \(M\), the reinsurer pays all above \(M\), if needed:

\[ Y=\begin{cases} X, & \text{if } X\leq M,\\ M, & \text{if } X>M; \end{cases} \qquad Z=X-Y. \tag{2.24}\]

Then \[ m_{k,Y} = \int_0^M x^k f_X(x)\,dx + M^k \mathbb{P}(X>M), \tag{2.25}\] \[ M_Y(t)= \int_0^M e^{tx}f_X(x)\,dx +e^{tM}\mathbb{P}(X>M), \tag{2.26}\] \[ m_{k,Z} = \int_M^\infty (x-M)^k f_X(x)\,dx, \tag{2.27}\] \[ M_Z(t) = \mathbb{P}(X\leq M) +\int_M^\infty e^{t(x-M)}f_X(x)\,dx. \tag{2.28}\]

2.11 Reinsurer’s view

In the conditions of the previous example, the reinsurer pays \(Z\), evidently, only if \(Z=X-Y>0\), otherwise, the reinsurer even does not know that a claim happened.

Hence, the distribution function for reinsurer is

\[ \begin{align} F_Z(z)&=\mathbb{P}(Z\leq z\mid Z>0)=\mathbb{P}(X\leq z+M\mid X>M)\\ &= \frac{\mathbb{P}(M<X\leq z+M)}{\mathbb{P}(X>M)}= \frac{F_X(z+M)-F_X(M)}{1-F_X(M)}. \end{align} \]

Then

\[ f_Z(z)= \frac{f_X(z+M)}{1-F_X(M)}, \quad z>0. \]

2.12 Agrregated claims with reinsurance

  • The aggregated claim \(S=X_1+\ldots+X_N\) of a collective risk model can be done when the reinsurance is in force.

  • Consider the case when the reinsurance is applied to each individual claim.

  • Let \(Y_i\) be the amount paid by the main insurer for the claim \(X_i\), and \(Z_i\) be the claim paid by the reinsurer, so \(X_i=Y_i+Z_i\).

  • Let \(S_I=Y_1+\ldots+Y_N\) be the aggregated claim amount paid by the insurer, and \(S_R=Z_1+\ldots+Z_N\) be the aggregated claim amount paid by the reinsurer.

  • Of course, formulas (2.4)–(2.6) are still true if we replace there \(S,X\) by \(S_I,Y\) or by \(S_R,Z\), respectively.

  • Note that, for the individual excess of loss reinsurance, the reinsurer pays \(Z_i\) only if \(Z_i=X_i-M>0\). We can introduce \(J_i=1\) if \(X_i>M\) and \(J_i=0\) otherwise. Then \(N_R:=J_1+\ldots+J_N\) is the number of claims paid by the reinsurer.

  • \(X_1,\ldots,X_N\) are i.i.d.r.v., let

    \[ \mathbb{P}(J_i=1)=\mathbb{P}(X_i>M)=:\rho. \]

    Then (actually \(J_i\sim Bin(1,\rho)\))

    \[ M_{J_i}(t)=\mathbb{E}(e^{t J_i})= \rho e^t +(1-p)e^0 = \rho e^t +1-p, \]

    and for the compound random variable \(N_R\),

    \[ M_{N_R}(t)=M_N\bigl(\ln (\rho e^t +1-p)\bigr). \tag{2.29}\]

  • Let \(\{i_1,\ldots,i_{N_R}\}\subset\{1,\ldots, N\}\) be indexes such that \(X_{i_k}>M\) for \(1\leq k\leq N_R\), and set \(W_k:=X_{i_k}-M\). Then there is another representation

    \[ S_R = W_1+\ldots + W_{N_R}. \tag{2.30}\]

  • If, additionally, \(N\sim Po(\lambda)\) for a \(\lambda>0\), then (2.29) reads, by (1.86) or (2.12),

    \[ M_{N_R}(t) =\exp\Bigl(\lambda \bigl(M_{J_i}(t)-1\bigr)\Bigr) =\exp\bigl(\rho\lambda (e^t-1)\bigr), \]

    i.e. \(N_R\sim Po(\rho\lambda)\), by (1.86) (the thinning property of the Poisson distribution.)
