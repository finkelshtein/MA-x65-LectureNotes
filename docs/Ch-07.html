7  Survival models

6.1 Main objects of study and notations

  • The future lifetime of a newborn person is a continuous random variable \(T:\Omega\to[0,\mathfrak{w} ]\), where $ $ is the limiting age.

  • Distribution function of \(T\) is denoted \[ F(t):=\mathbb{P}(T\leq t), \qquad t\geq0. \]

  • Survival function of \(T\) is \[ S(t):=\mathbb{P}(T>t)=1-F(t), \qquad t\geq0. \]

  • The future lifetime after age \(x\), for \(x\in[0,\mathfrak{w} ]\), is a random variable \(T_x:\Omega\to [0,\mathfrak{w} -x]\) (i.e. \(T=x+T_x\)). In particular, \(T_0=T\).

  • Distribution function of \(T_x\) is denoted \[ F_x(t) :=\mathbb{P}(T_x\leq t). \]

  • Survival function of \(T_x\) is denoted \[ S_x(t) :=\mathbb{P}(T_x> t)=1-F_x(t). \]

  • Relation between \(F_x\) and \(F\) (or \(S\)): \[ \begin{aligned} F_x(t)&=\mathbb{P}(T\leq x+t\mid T>x)=\frac{\mathbb{P}(x<T\leq x+t)}{\mathbb{P}(T>x)}\\ & =\frac{F(x+t)-F(x)}{S(x)}=\frac{F(x+t)-F(x)}{1-F(x)}. \end{aligned} \tag{7.1}\]

6.2 Actuarial notations

  • \({}_{t}q_{x}:= F_x(t)\)

  • \({}_{t}p_{x}:=1-{}_{t}q_{x}=S_x(t)\)

  • \(q_{x}:={}_{1}q_{x}\)

  • \(p_{x}:={}_{1}p_{x}\)

6.3 Survival probabilities relations

  • We have \[ \begin{aligned} S_x(t) &= \mathbb{P}(T_x>t) = \mathbb{P}(T>x+t\mid T>x)\\ &=\frac{\mathbb{P}(T>x+t)}{\mathbb{P}(T>x)}=\frac{S(x+t)}{S(x)}. \end{aligned} \tag{7.2}\]

  • In actuarial notations, \[ {}_{t}p_{x}=\frac{{}_{x+t}p_{0}}{{}_{x}p_{0}}. \]

  • Therefore, \[ {}_{s+t}p_{x}=\frac{{}_{x+s+t}p_{0}}{{}_{x}p_{0}}=\frac{{}_{x+s}p_{0}}{{}_{x}p_{0}}\cdot \frac{{}_{x+s+t}p_{0}}{{}_{x+s}p_{0}}={}_{s}p_{x}\cdot {}_{t}p_{x+s}. \] As a result, \[ {}_{s+t}p_{x}={}_{s}p_{x}\cdot {}_{t}p_{x+s} ={}_{t}p_{x}\cdot {}_{s}p_{x+t}. \tag{7.3}\]

  • This (obvious) property is called the consistency condition.

6.4 The force of mortality

  • The force of mortality \(\mu_x\) represents the instantaneous rate of mortality at a certain age \(x\in[0,\mathfrak{w} ]\): \[ \mu_x = \lim_{h\to0} \frac1h \mathbb{P}(T\leq x+h\mid T>x). \tag{7.4}\]

  • By (7.1), \(F_x(0)=0\) and \[ \begin{aligned} \mu_x &= \lim_{h\to0} \frac1h \bigl( F_x(h)-F_x(0) \bigr)\\ &=F_x'(0)=\dfrac{d}{dt} \bigl( {}_{t}q_{x} \bigr)\biggr\rvert_{t=0}. \end{aligned} \tag{7.5}\]

  • Consider the probability density function of \(T\): \[ f(t):=F'(t). \tag{7.6}\] Then, one gets from (7.1): \[ \mu_x = \frac{F'(x)}{1-F(x)}= \frac{f(x)}{1-F(x)}. \tag{7.7}\]

  • The term “force of mortality” is mainly used in actuarial science. In engineering, the term failure rate is in use, and in statistics, it is called the hazard function.

  • Since \(S(x)=1-F(x)\), one can rewrite further: \[ \mu_x=-\, \frac{S'(x)}{S(x)}=-\, \frac{d}{dx} \bigl( \ln S(x) \bigr). \tag{7.8}\]

6.5 Remark

The following interpretation may be useful: since \(F_x(h)={}_{h}q_{x}\), we have \[ {}_{h}q_{x}\approx \mu_x \cdot h. \tag{7.9}\]

6.6 Probability density function of \(T_x\)

  • We consider \[ f_x(t)=\dfrac{d}{dt} F_x(t). \tag{7.10}\]

  • We have then, by (7.1), \[ \begin{aligned} f_x(t)& = \dfrac{d}{dt} \frac{F(x+t)-F(x)}{S(x)}= \frac{F'(x+t)}{S(x)}\\ & = \frac{F'(x+t)}{S(x+t)}\frac{S(x+t)}{S(x)} \end{aligned} \] and since \(F'(y)=1-S'(y)\), we can use (7.8) and (7.2) to continue: \[ \begin{aligned} &=-\frac{S'(x+t)}{S(x+t)}S_x(t)\\& =S_x(t)\mu_{x+t}, \end{aligned} \] i.e., for \(0\leq t\leq \mathfrak{w} -x\), \[ f_x(t)=S_x(t)\mu_{x+t}={}_{t}p_{x} \cdot \mu_{x+t}. \tag{7.11}\]

From (7.11), we have \[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^t {}_{s}p_{x} \cdot \mu_{x+s}\,ds&= \int_0^t f_x(s)\,ds = \int_0^t \dfrac{d}{dt} F_x(s)\,ds\\ & = F_x(t)-F_x(0) =F_x(t); \end{aligned} \] i.e. \[ {}_{t}q_{x}= \int_0^t {}_{s}p_{x} \cdot \mu_{x+s}\,ds. \tag{7.12}\]

6.7 Integral expressions

We found in (7.12) an integral expression for \({}_{t}q_{x}\). Consider now another one, for \({}_{t}p_{x}\). By (7.8), \[ \mu_{x+s} = -\frac{S'(x+s)}{S(x+s)} =-\frac{d}{ds} \ln S(x+s), \] hence, integrating in \(s\in[0,t]\) and using (7.2), \[ \begin{aligned} -\int_0^t \mu_{x+s}\,ds &= \Bigl[\ln S(x+s)\Bigr]_0^t=\ln \frac{S(x+t)}{S(x)}\\ & = \ln S_x(t)=\ln{}_{t}p_{x}. \end{aligned} \] Therefore, \[ {}_{t}p_{x} = \exp\biggl(-\int_0^t \mu_{x+s}\,ds \biggr). \tag{7.13}\]

6.8 Remark

Formula (7.13) can be also obtained by considering a time-non-homogeneous MJP with states \(1\), , and \(2\), Dead, and the transition rate \(\mu_t\). Then \[ {}_{t}p_{x}=p_{\overline{1,1}}(x,x+t)=\exp\biggl(-\int_{x}^{x+t}\mu_s\,ds\biggr), \] that is nothing but (7.13), by a change of variables.

6.9 Life table functions

  • \(l_x\) denotes the expected number of lives at (integer) age \(x\in[0,\mathfrak{w}]\).

  • \(d_x\) is the expected number of deaths between the ages of \(x\) and \(x+1\).

  • The following formulas holds: \[ d_x= l_x-l_{x+1}; \tag{7.14}\]

\[ p_x=\dfrac{l_{x+1}}{l_x}; \tag{7.15}\]

\[ q_x=1-p_x=1-\dfrac{l_{x+1}}{l_x}=\dfrac{d_x}{l_x}; \tag{7.16}\]

\[ {}_{t}p_{x}= \dfrac{l_{x+t}}{l_x}; \tag{7.17}\]

\[ {}_{t}q_{x} = 1-{}_{t}p_{x}=\dfrac{l_x-l_{x+t}}{l_x}. \tag{7.18}\]

6.10 Justification of formula (7.18)

Let \(Y_x\) be the random variable of the alive individuals of age \(x\), so that \[ l_x=\mathbb{E}(Y_x), \quad x\in \mathbb{Z}_+. \] Then, by the property of the conditional expectation \[ l_{x+1}=\mathbb{E}(Y_{x+1})=\mathbb{E}\bigl( \mathbb{E}(Y_{x+1} \mid Y_x=l_x) \bigr). \] Next, \(Z_x:=\mathbb{E}(Y_{x+1} \mid Y_x=l_x)\) is a random variable which satisfies the binomial law: there are \(l_x\) individuals of age \(x\) each of them may survive in the next year (independently from others) with probability \(p_x\) and die with probability \(q_x=1-p_x\), i.e. e.g. \[ \mathbb{P}(Z_x = k) = \binom{l_x}{k} (p_x)^k (q_x)^{l_x-k}. \] We know from Probability course that the expectation of a binomial random variable is the product of the number of possible trials and the probability of “success”, therefore, \[ l_{x+1}=\mathbb{E}(Y_{x+1}) = \mathbb{E} (Z_x) = l_x p_x, \] that implies (7.18).

6.11 Theorem: Uniform distribution of deaths

If deaths are uniformly distributed between the ages of \(x\) and \(x+1\), then, for all \(t\in[0,1]\): \[ {}_{t}q_{x}=t \cdot q_{x}. \tag{7.19}\]

Proof. Since the number of deaths is uniformly distributed, \(l_{x+t}\in[l_{x+1},l_x]\) for all \(t\in[0,1]\) and \(l_{x+t}= (1-t)l_{x}+t l_{x+1}\),

therefore, \[ {}_{t}q_{x}=\dfrac{l_x-l_{x+t}}{l_x}=\dfrac{t l_x - tl_{x+1}}{l_x} = t(1-p_x)=tq_x, \] that proves the statement.

6.12 Remark: Constant force of mortality assumption

Another assumption for the ages between \(x\) and \(x+1\) is that the force of mortality remains constant: \[ \mu_{x+s} = \mu_x, \quad s\in[0,1). \tag{7.20}\] In this case, by (7.13), one has \[ {}_{t}p_{x} = e^{-t\mu_x} = \bigl( e^{-1\cdot \mu_x}\bigr)^t =(p_x)^t. \tag{7.21}\] Therefore, \[ {}_{t}q_{x}=1 - e^{-t\mu_x} \neq t (1-e^{-\mu_x})= t q_x. \tag{7.22}\] However, if \(\mu_x\approx 0\), then \({}_{t}q_{x}\approx t\mu_x \approx t q_x\).

6.13 Analogue of consistency condition

The consistency condition (7.3) is actually nothing but the conditional probability identity. It’s counterpart for \({}_{t}q_{x}\) reads as follows: \[ {}_{t+s}q_{x}= {}_{s}q_{x}+{}_{s}p_{x}\cdot{}_{t}q_{x+s}. \tag{7.23}\] The interpretations is as follows: to die within the next \(t+s\) years the person may either die in the next \(s\) years or survive in the next \(s\) years but die within next \(t\) years thereafter.

Formula (7.23) can be derived rigorously from the law of the full probability (do this by yourself!) or just from (7.1): \[ \begin{aligned} {}_{t+s}q_{x}&= \frac{F(x+t+s)-F(x)}{1-F(x)}\\ &= \frac{F(x+t+s)-F(x+s)}{1-F(x)} +\frac{F(x+s)-F(x)}{1-F(x)}\\ &= \frac{F(x+t+s)-F(x+s)}{1-F(x+s)}\frac{1-F(x+s)}{1-F(x)} \\&\quad +\frac{F(x+s)-F(x)}{1-F(x)}\\ & = {}_{t}q_{x+s}\cdot{}_{x}p_{s} + {}_{x}q_{s}, \end{aligned} \] where we used that, by (7.2), \[ \frac{1-F(x+s)}{1-F(x)} = \frac{S(x+s)}{S(x)}={}_{s}p_{x}. \]

6.14 Central rate of mortality

The central rate of mortality at age \(x\) is the ration of the number of deaths over the year of age \(x\) to \(x+1\) to the average number of lives then: \[ m_x=\dfrac{d_x}{\displaystyle\int_0^1 l_{x+t}\,dt}. \tag{7.24}\] We can also rewrite this: \[ m_x= \frac{q_x}{\displaystyle\int_0^1 {}_{t}p_{x} \,dt}= \dfrac{\displaystyle\int_0^1 {}_{t}p_{x} \mu_{x+t}\,dt}{\displaystyle\int_0^1 {}_{t}p_{x} \,dt}<q_x.{} \tag{7.25}\]

6.15 Complete expectation of life

The expected future lifetime after age \(x\in[0,\mathfrak{w}]\) is defined as follows: \[ \mathring{e}_x: = \mathbb{E}(T_x). \tag{7.26}\] Using the definition of the expectation for a continuous random variable, we have, by (7.11), \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}(T_x)&= \int_0^{\mathfrak{w}-x} t f_x(t)\,dt\\ & = \int_0^{\mathfrak{w}-x} t \cdot \dfrac{d}{dt} F_x(t)\,dt \\ &= -\int_0^{\mathfrak{w}-x} t \cdot \dfrac{d}{dt} {}_{t}p_{x} \,dt \end{aligned} \] and then we integrate by parts \[ \begin{aligned} &=- \Bigl[t {}_{t}p_{x} \Bigr]_0^{\mathfrak{w}-x}+ \int_0^{\mathfrak{w}-x} {}_{t}p_{x} \,dt\\ &= \int_0^{\mathfrak{w}-x} {}_{t}p_{x} \,dt, \end{aligned} \] as $ {}{-x}p{x}=(T_{x}>-x)=0$.

Since \[ {}_{t}p_{x}=0 \quad\text{for } t>\mathfrak{w}-x \] as well, one can rewrite: \[ \mathring{e}_x = \mathbb{E}(T_x)=\int_0^\infty {}_{t}p_{x} \,dt. \tag{7.27}\]

6.16 Theorem: Monotonicity of the lifetime

Function \(x+\mathring{e}_x\) is increasing in \(x\), i.e. \[ x+\mathring{e}_x<y+\mathring{e}_y, \quad x<y. \tag{7.28}\] In other words, expectation for the age of death \(T=x+T_x\) increases when the person managed to live more.

Proof. We have \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dx}\mathring{e}_x &= \int_0^\infty \frac{d}{dx} {}_{t}p_{x}\,dt= \int_0^\infty \frac{d}{dx} \frac{S(x+t)}{S(x)}\,dt\\ & = \int_0^\infty \frac{S'(x+t)S(x)-S'(x)S(x+t)}{(S(x))^2}\,dt\\ & =\int_0^\infty \frac{S'(x+t)}{S(x)}\,dt +\mu_x\int_0^\infty \frac{S(x+t)}{S(x)}\,dt \\ & = -1+\mu_x \mathring{e}_x. \end{aligned} \] Therefore, \[ \frac{d}{dx} \left( \mathring{e}_x + x \right) = \mu_x \mathring{e}_x>0. \]

6.17 Remark

The previous statement may be confusing if we look at equalities \(T=x+T_x\) and \(T=y+T_y\), so then we would expect that $ (T)=x+_x$ and \(\mathbb{E}(T)=y+\mathring{e}_y\). Apparently, these are two different random variables both (actually, wrongly) denoted by \(T\). Namely, when we write \(T=x+T_x\), we consider the random lifetime \(T\) conditional that a person was alive at age \(x\), it is measurable w.r.t. \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by ages \(\leq x\). The random variable \(\tilde{T}=y+T_y\) is, hence, different.

6.18 Curtate expectation of life

The curtate future lifetime of a life age \(x\) is \[ K_x=[T_x], \] where \([y]\) denotes the integer part of \(y\), i.e. \(y\) is rounded down to the nearest integer.

We have then, because of the continuity of \(T\) (i.e. of \(F(t)\)), \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{P}(K_x=k)& =\mathbb{P}(k\leq T_x<k+1)\\ &=\mathbb{P}(k<T_x\leq k+1)\\&={}_{k}p_{x}\cdot q_{x+k}. \end{aligned} \] We define the curtate expectation of life as follows: \[ e_x=\mathbb{E}(K_x). \tag{7.29}\] We have, hence, \[ \begin{aligned} e_x &= \sum_{k=0}^{[\mathfrak{w}-x]} k\cdot \mathbb{P}(K_x=k) =\sum_{k=1}^{[\mathfrak{w}-x]} \sum_{j=1}^k 1\cdot \mathbb{P}(K_x=k)\\ &=\sum_{j=1}^{[\mathfrak{w}-x]}\sum_{k=j}^{[\mathfrak{w}-x]}\mathbb{P}(K_x=k)= \sum_{j=1}^{[\mathfrak{w}-x]}\mathbb{P}(K_x\geq j). \end{aligned} \] Since \(\mathbb{P}(K_x\geq j)= \mathbb{P}(T_x\geq j)={}_{j}p_{x}\) (\(=0\) for \(j>[\mathfrak{w}-x]\)), \[ e_x =\mathbb{E}(K_x) =\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}{}_{j}p_{x}. \tag{7.30}\]

6.19 Simple parametric survival models

  • Exponential model : \[ \mu_x=\mu, \quad \text{for all } x\in[0,\mathfrak{w}]. \] Then \[ {}_{t}p_{x}=S_x(t)=e^{-\mu t} \] Note that \[ f(t) = S(t)\mu_t = \mu e^{-\mu t}. \]

  • Weibull model : \[ \mu_x=\alpha \beta x^{\beta-1}, \quad \alpha, \beta>0. \] Note that \(\mu_x\) is increasing if \(\beta>1\) and decreasing if \(\beta\in(0,1)\). \(\beta=1\) corresponds to the exponential model. We have \[ {}_{t}p_{x}=S_x(t)=\exp\Bigl( - \alpha \bigl((x+t)^\beta -x^\beta\bigr) \Bigr). \] In particular, \[ S(t)=e^{- \alpha t^\beta}, \] hence, \[ f(t) = \alpha \beta t^{\beta-1}e^{- \alpha t^\beta}. \] Parameter \(\beta\) is called shape and \(\alpha\) is called scale.

  • Gompertz model : \[ \mu_x= b c^x, \quad b,c>0. \]

If \(c>1\), then \(\mu_x\) is increasing; and if \(c\in(0,1)\), then \(\mu_x\) is decreasing. \(c=1\) corresponds to the exponential model.

Parameter \(b>0\) is called rate. Often the hazard rate is written for \(c=e^\gamma\), where \(\gamma=\ln c\in\mathbb{R}\) is called shape.

We have \[ {}_{t}p_{x} = S_x(t)=g ^{c^x(c^t-1)}, \] where \[ g=\exp\Bigl(-\frac{b}{\ln c}\Bigr). \]

We have also: \[ f(t) = \mu_t S(t)= bc^t g^{c^t-1}. \]

  • Gompertz–Makeham model : \[ \mu_x= a+b c^x, \quad a,b,c>0. \] Then \[ {}_{t}p_{x} =S_x(t)=e^{-at} g ^{c^x(c^t-1)}, \] where \(g\) is the same as above. Then \[ f(t) = (a+b c^t) e^{-at} g ^{c^t-1}. \]
